On Taking
Things Apart

A personal account of the engineer who cannot leave well enough alone — on version control, glass boxes, and the patience it takes to watch a model lie to you.

by Santosh Cheethirala · Bengaluru · 2026

There is a specific pleasure in the moment before you understand something — the moment when the machine sits there, doing exactly what it was told, and you have no idea why. I have spent most of my life engineering that moment deliberately, then engineering my way out of it. I open things. I opened the family television when I was nine. It did not work afterward, but I knew, for the first time, what a transformer was. The urge has never left. If anything, it has found better targets.

Version control is, on its surface, a solved problem. Git exists. Git works. Nobody sensible rebuilds Git. But sensible was never quite the point — understanding was. So I spent a semester writing pes-vcs1, a version-control system in C: a .pes/ object store, SHA-256 content-addressed blobs, a staging index that negotiated between working tree and history, commit graphs walked by parent pointers. When you implement something yourself, you stop treating it as a black box you trust and start treating it as a system you can reason about. Every abstraction becomes load-bearing. Every shortcut becomes visible. The exercise taught me more about software than any lecture on software design, and I am not saying that to be clever.

The same logic pushed me toward machine learning, except the stakes are different. A broken version-control system is merely inconvenient. A model that is confidently wrong about something consequential is a different kind of problem entirely. I kept encountering the same opaque surface: the model knows, or it doesn't, and you have no principled way to tell which. That bothered me more than I could articulate for a while, and eventually I stopped trying to articulate it and started building instead. The result was GlassBox2 — a mechanistic-interpretability visualizer for GPT-2 internals. Activation patching. All 144 attention heads, live. I wanted to see the fact.

And you can. The "Paris is the capital of France" fact sits in layer 0. When you patch the activation there, the capital logit flips: +1.89 → −2.81. The model stops knowing. It is not mystical. It is arithmetic at a specific index in a specific residual stream, and once you see it you cannot unsee it. That is the project, really: making the invisible arithmetic visible. I want to build tools that let you point at a model and say, here — this is where it decided.

Local-first follows from the same conviction. If a tool depends on a server I don't control, it is a black box I rent. My data goes in; outputs come out; I have no account of what happened in between. The alternative is: your data, your GPU, no intermediary. Gradient3 is the sharpest statement of this I've managed — a neural-network trainer that runs entirely in the browser, on your GPU, using raw WGSL compute shaders. No server. No dependencies. 97% MNIST from a two-layer MLP, with every kernel verified against an f64 CPU oracle. The point is not the accuracy number. The point is that nothing about it is hidden from you.

I shot 10m air rifle at the national level for three years. People who haven't done precision shooting assume it must be about steadiness — keeping still. It isn't, really. It's about knowing exactly what your body does when it isn't trying to do anything, and working with that rather than against it. The parallels to debugging are obvious enough that I'll resist spelling them out, except to say that the methodology is the same: reduce variables until there is one left, then address that one. Don't hurry the trigger. Don't hurry the stack trace. Orbit4 — the local focus engine I maintain — has a scheduling loop that runs in under 5ms. That number exists because I was slow, then I was methodical, then I was fast.

This summer I am joining PI Labs as a research intern, working on hallucination attribution in RAG pipelines. The question is not whether a model hallucinates — they do, reliably, when the retrieval goes wrong. The question is which retrieved chunk introduced the error. That is a localization problem. That is, in exactly the same sense as activation patching, the question of pointing at a system and saying: here. The tools change; the impulse doesn't. I am still opening the television. It still doesn't work afterward. But now I know why.

If there is a theme, it is this: I would rather understand one system deeply than use ten systems shallowly. The tools I build are evidence of that preference. So is the time I spend taking apart the tools I didn't build.
